Dēvanāgarī Letters for the Transcription of Tamiḻ

प्रणौमि रुद्रं डमरुध्वनन्तम्।
स्कन्दं च देवं ग्रहमातृकेशम्॥
ऋषिं त्वगस्त्यं गुहशिष्यमार्यम्।
तथा च काव्यं तृणबिन्दुमीडे॥

Introduction

Tamiḻ texts are often rendered in Dēvanāgarī for the convenience of readers more familiar with that script, whether they are native speakers of Hindī, Marāṭhī, and other languages, or Tamiḻ speakers who grew up reading Dēvanāgarī.

However, the Dēvanāgarī script in its current form has certain limitations in its ability to transcribe Tamiḻ faithfully and legibly. In what follows, we show how the reshaping of a few existing letters, the introduction of a few new ones, and the functional extension of one letter can help achieve transcription that is faithful, aesthetic, and legible, while accommodating multiple transcription schemes. To make these additions practically usable, they have also been incorporated into a modified version of the Shobhika font, released as Shobhika Dravida.

Redesigned Letters

At present, Dēvanāgarī includes three nuqtaed variant characters used for Tamiḻ transcription, namely ऴ, ऱ, and ऩ, which correspond to Tamiḻ ழ, ற, and ன respectively. The use of nuqtaed letters in these cases presents two issues. First, the letters are easily mistaken for their unnuqtaed counterparts. Second, the frequent appearance of nuqtas makes a text look cluttered and hampers legibility.

To address this problem, these three letters have been redesigned as ऴ, ऱ, and ऩ respectively.

New Consonant Characters

त़ and द़ for Tamiḻ ற

In Old Tamiḻ (and in southern dialects of modern Tamiḻ), the letter ற is realized in three different ways:

  • as an unvoiced alveolar stop [t] when geminated, as in ற்ற,
  • as a voiced alveolar stop [d] in ன்ற, and
  • as an alveolar trill [r] otherwise.

In most modern dialects, however, ற்ற and ன்ற are realized as [tr] and [nr], or with an epenthetic stop as [ttr] and [ndr].

While ऱ would suffice for all these allophones in a strict transliteration, a phonetically accurate representation (useful for readers not versed in Tamiḻ orthography) requires separate characters for [t] and [d]. These have accordingly been introduced as त़ and द़.

The variant consonant clusters ற்ற and ன்ற may thus be transliterated or transcribed as follows:

श़ for Tamiḻ ச

The letter ச is similarly realised in three different ways:

  • as च word-initially and when geminated,
  • as ज following a nasal, and
  • as either स or श intervocalically, depending on the speaker.

This intervocalic pronunciation presents an interesting conundrum in transcription. One must choose either स or श and impose that pronunciation on the reader. Such rigidity may be desirable in certain contexts (for example, when adhering to a tradition of chanting or transcribing a specific individual's speech). However, some may wish to preserve the ambiguity, especially when स and श of Sanskritic origin coexist in the same text.

To accommodate this ambiguity in intervocalic ச, the letter श़ has been introduced, providing an optional representation that allows the reader to select the pronunciation rather than it being prescribed.

New Vowel Characters

ऐॅ (ैॅ) for ai-kāra-kuṟukkam

The Tamiḻ vowel ஐ has an allophonic variant known as the ai-kāra-kuṟukkam. This variant is described as being prosodically shorter in duration than the regular ஐ. In some modern colloquial dialects, the ai-kāra-kuṟukkam merges with word-final ஏ, yielding a distinct vowel that cannot be straightforwardly characterized as either அ or எ. A new vowel character ऐॅ (ैॅ) is introduced to give the option of marking this explicitly, particularly for grammatical purposes and metrical analysis.

Modified and Extended Characters

ஃ for Tamiḻ āytam

Tamiḻ has an archaic phoneme known as the āytam (ஃ). Many transliterators today substitute the āytam with the visarga (ः), but this equivalence is far from perfect, as the āytam is pronounced distinctly from the visarga. To enable separate transcription, the existing Tamiḻ āytam character has been stylistically modified for Dēvanāgarī as ஃ.

ॶ (ॖ) for kuṟṟiyal-u-karam

Finally, Tamiḻ உ has an allophone known as the kuṟṟiyal-u-karam. This is distinctly pronounced as [ɨ], and although the Tamiḻ script does not mark it separately, a separate marking in Dēvanāgarī is very helpful for non-native speakers. Fortunately, this same sound exists in Kaśmīrī, where there is already a standard Dēvanāgarī letter for it: ॶ (ॖ). We may therefore extend its use to represent the kuṟṟiyal-u-karam of Tamiḻ as well.

Conclusion

In total, the addition of these eight new letters will enable more aesthetic and legible methods of transcribing Tamiḻ into Dēvanāgarī and will aid the dissemination and wider appreciation of Tamiḻ texts across the rest of India.
